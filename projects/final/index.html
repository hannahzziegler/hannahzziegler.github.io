School districts in 26 states have banned or opened investigations into books they believe teach innappropriate topics to students, according to an April report from PEN America, an advocacy organization that compiled data on nationwide book bans over the last 14 months.

In total, PEN America lists more than 1,500 instances of individual books being banned. The bans affect students in 26 states and impact more than 1,100 unique book titles. The index includes books that have been banned across 86 school districts that enroll over 2 million students.

Among the 26 states with documented bans, Texas leads in both the number of districts that have enacted bans and the highest number of individual bans. The state has banned 713 individual titles since March 2021, according to PEN.

The organization counted actions that lead to the removal of books for at least a day and found that Florida, Pennsylvania and Texas accounted for the vast majority of book bans across the county. The three states have had more than 1,300 instances of book bans, or 87 percent of the roughly 1,500 banning incidents that PEN America tallied.

More than 40 percent of the bans are tied to directives from elected lawmakers to investigate or remove books. These directives include an October request from a Republican state lawmaker in Texas that sent a list of 850 books to school districts in the state and asked officials to investigate “material that might make students feel discomfort, guilt, anguish, or any other form of psychological distress because of their race or sex.”

A Changing Legislative Landscape

Other bans have resulted from legislation that bars schools from teaching students about topics related to diversity and equity, such as the study of racism and inequality, as well as issued surrounding sexuality and gender identity.

A Florida law signed in March is among the most high profile examples of legislators intervening in school curriculum. The law – which critics have dubbed the “Don’t Say Gay” bill – prohibits classroom instruction about sexual orientation and gender identity in the state’s public schools.

Another law passed in Tennessee will require school libraries to determine whether books are age appropriate, while a law pending in Georgia would give principals the power to remove “obscene” books from schools.

The legislative and school board actions that have been taken against certain books spiked in late 2021 and early 2021. More than half of all book banning incidents that PEN America tallied happened in September and December of 2021, coinciding with legislative implementation of aforementioned laws in Tennessee and Texas.

Attacks on LGBTQ+ Characters and Stories

In the most recent wave of book banning across the country, school administrators have taken aim at books that confront LGBTQ+ representation, particularly those that touch on topics of gender identity and queerness. The most banned books in the nation each confront themes of race, queerness and the frequent overlap between these identities.

Of the titles that PEN America indexed, 33 percent explicitly address LGBTQ+ themes or have protagonists or prominent secondary characters who are members of the LGBTQ+ community. Another 107 titles have themes related to rights and activism.

Many of the most banned books across the nation explicitly confront gender and sexuality. The most banned book – "Gender Queer: A Memoir" by Maia Kobabe – is a graphic novel that started as a way for the author to describe to eir family what it means to be gender queer and asexual.

Simon and Schuester described Kobabe’s work as “a useful and touching guide on gender identity—what it means and how to think about it.” But the book has been challenged 30 times across the country in the last year.

In November, Texas Governor Greg Abbott sent a letter to the Texas Association of School Boards asking it to remove what he described as “pornographic” materials from school districts. In calling on the state to develop standards for evaluation, Abbott singled out "Gender Queer" for its content.

"Removing or restricting queer books in libraries and schools is like cutting a lifeline for queer youth," Maia Kobabe, author of Gender Queer, told The Washington Post.

"Keeping these stories, away from [youth] doesn’t stop them from going through the same situations," George M. Johnson, author of All Boys Aren’t Blue, told PENPod.

"Whether people read my books is their choice. Only their choice, only for themselves,”said Susan Kuklin, author of "Beyond Magenta."

Some experts see the current attack on books by and for the LGBTQ+ community as particularly troubling. Shannon Neal, a first year graduate student at the University of Maryland and graduate assistant at the university library’s comparative studies center, said banning books with identity-based topics fosters learned intolerance for students.

“It’s just very limiting to students. Having [these books] off the library shelves means you can’t just discover topics of racism, misogyny, antisemitism,” they said. “It also just creates this atmosphere of taboo, which doesn’t help with academic freedom or learning.”

When a book is taken out of the classroom, it usually takes place when students are in the middle of reading it, Neal said. Students suffer from not getting to have a full lesson plan surrounding the challenging topics of some books when they are removed, which increases the chance they could develop misconceptions about taboo topics like sexualiy and gender queerness, they added.

The Long History of Racial Unrest and Literature

Other books have been banned because of what Neal calls “anti-white” and “anti-police” messaging. One prominent banned book – “The Hate U Give” by Angie Thomas – depicts racism, classism and opens with a scene of police violence.

Neal said she was disappointed, but not surprised at the recent wave of book banning that has taken hold across the nation in the last year.

“We all feel the political atmosphere right now, And I don't know how surprising it is to anyone, honestly. It's deeply disappointing,” Neal said. “I am definitely concerned. I don't know where we're heading.”

Books with themes of race and class have also helped students grapple with the country’s history of racism and slavery for more than a century, said Tahirah Akbar-Williams, the librarian for the University of Maryland’s schools of education and African American studies.

Growing up in an all-white neighborhood, Akbar-Williams did not have candid conversations with her classmates about her blackness until a teacher had her class read Harper Lee’s "To Kill A Mockingbird" – a book that has been challenged and banned dozens of times since its release in 1960.

“That book was pivotal in helping people understand racism, racial relations, violence against black bodies, and identity,” Akbar-Williams said. “It's important to include that perspective in K-12 learning and have those discussions that inform citizens and give them a better perspective and understanding of the country they live in.”

Many districts have banned both fiction and nonfiction books like “The Hate U Give,” and “How to be an Antiracist” out of fear their children would be indoctrinated into a mindset of Critical Race Theory – an idea that race is a social construct, and that racism is not merely the product of individual bias, but also something embedded in the fabric of the nation. Book banning contributes to misinformed ideas around the nation about racial justice initiatives and Critical Race Theory itself, Akbar-Williams said.

“I saw a guy on TV, he said ‘I just don’t want my kid to feel bad about being white.’ And I thought that’s not critical race theory, and that’s problematic,” Akbar-Williams said. “And so if you banned the book, how will people ever know what critical race theory is?”

I think the same sensibilities that informed those people to make it a criminal act for Black people to read are the ancestors of the same people who are making it a criminal act for their own children to read and I don’t see a great deal of difference between that. There is some hysteria associated with the idea of reading that is all out of proportion.


-Nobel Prize laureate Toni Morrison, on book banning, 1982

Of the titles in the index, 41 percent contain protagonists or prominent secondary characters of color, and 22 percent directly address issues of race and racism. Books like "Out of Darkness" by Ashley Hope Perez – which is about an interracial relationship in 1930s Texas – also overlap with censorship categories that cover topics of sex and relationships.

In the index, 283 unique titles – including Perez’s, which is the fourth most-banned book nationwide – contain sexual content of varying kinds. This includes novels with sexual encounters, as well as informational books about puberty, sex and relationships.

“Book challengers may convince themselves that they want to save the kids. But it’s a damaging myth that removing a story about painful aspects of human experience will in any way protect young people. This is like arguing that a school-wide moratorium on discussions of bullying will eliminate the problem. Silence is the real threat.”


- Ashley Hope Pérez, author of award-winning Out of Darkness, Dallas Morning News, 2022

Akbar-Williams sees the nationwide push to outlaw books about underrepresented groups as a backlash against social justice progress that has been made since the death of George Floyd in 2020 and the legalization of gay marriage.

Having books could help Americans to progress their political and social conversations toward a more equal and accepting society, but the overwhelming power of white supremacy in this country diminishes those conversations, Akbar-Williams said. The discomfort of lawmakers and school boards around sensitive issues will cause the nation’s book banning “crisis” to continue, she added.

“We won't even talk about it. Racism doesn't exist. That's what the parents are saying: ‘I don't want my white kids to be uncomfortable that my uncle or my brother is saying the N-word and killing black people in the car,’” Akbar-Williams said. “When power gets uncomfortable, it will strike back, no matter what.”
